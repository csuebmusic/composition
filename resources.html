resources

Organizations, funding, residencies, and opportunities for a working life in music.
Inés Thiebaut · CSU East Bay

A running list of organizations, funding, and opportunities for composers. Much of this is the professional side of a life in music that rarely comes up in a lesson: who to join, who pays for new work, and where to find the time and space to write.

before you apply

Read the eligibility and deadline for anything before you spend time on it. Many of these are open to students or early-career composers, and some fund only one specific kind of project, like a recording or a premiere. Most are based in the United States.

organizations to join

These are membership organizations for composers. Joining connects you to a field: other composers, calls for scores, newsletters, conferences, and sometimes performances or recordings of your work. Many are free or inexpensive for students, and a membership is one of the simplest ways to stop working in isolation. A few, like the American Composers Alliance, also publish and license their members' music.

  • American Composers Alliance (ACA): publishes and licenses members' concert music. Affiliation with a PRO is required.
  • American Composers Forum (ACF): a large US membership organization, with grants, programs, and the magazine I Care If You Listen.
  • Society of Composers, Inc. (SCI): composers in and around academia, with conferences and calls for scores.
  • National Association of Composers/USA (NACUSA): concerts and a dedicated student membership.
  • SEAMUS: the electroacoustic and computer-music community, with an annual conference.
  • International Society for Contemporary Music (ISCM): an international network, home of the World New Music Days festival.
  • International Alliance for Women in Music (IAWM): advocacy, competitions, and community for women in music.
  • College Music Society (CMS): a cross-disciplinary organization for music in higher education.
  • Asian Composers League: a regional network and festival across the Asia-Pacific.

rights and royalties

When your music is performed in public, broadcast, or streamed, you are owed a small payment called a performance royalty. A performing rights organization (PRO) collects those royalties and pays them to you: you affiliate with one, register your works, and they track performances and send what you are owed. If you expect your music to be played anywhere public, this is the first business step to take. SoundExchange is related but separate, collecting royalties specifically from digital and satellite radio.

  • ASCAP: a PRO you can join directly.
  • BMI: a PRO you can join directly.
  • SESAC: a PRO you join by invitation.
  • SoundExchange: collects digital performance royalties from streaming and satellite radio.

grants, commissions, and fellowships

This is where the money to make new work comes from, and the words matter. A commission pays you to write a specific new piece, usually for a particular performer or ensemble. A grant funds a project or activity you propose. A fellowship recognizes you or your work with money and time, often with no strings attached. Most have deadlines and an application, and many are open to students or early-career composers.

commissions and new work

  • New Music USA: project grants supporting the creation and performance of new work.
  • Chamber Music America: commissioning grants in classical and jazz.
  • EarShot (American Composers Orchestra): reading sessions where emerging composers workshop a new piece with a professional orchestra.
  • Fromm Music Foundation: annual commissions for new work in any idiom, including electronic and hybrid.
  • Koussevitzky Music Foundation: commissions, usually made with a performing organization.
  • Barlow Endowment: a general commissioning program and commissions in specific areas.
  • MAP Fund: supports live performance projects that experiment with form.

fellowships and awards

  • Guggenheim Fellowship: fellowships across the arts, including composition.
  • American Academy of Arts and Letters: annual music awards to composers.
  • American Academy in Rome: the Rome Prize, a residency-fellowship in Italy.
  • American Academy in Berlin: the Berlin Prize fellowship.
  • Creative Capital: project grants and professional support for original new work.
  • United States Artists: unrestricted fellowships to artists, including musicians.
  • Foundation for Contemporary Arts: grants to artists working in experimental traditions.

public funding

  • National Endowment for the Arts: the US federal arts funder.
  • California Arts Council: your state arts agency, worth knowing since you study in California.
  • New York State Council on the Arts: one example of a state arts council. Every state has its own.

recording

  • Aaron Copland Fund for Music: supports recording projects and organizations that present new music.

emergency and fiscal support

Two different safety nets. Emergency grants are fast, small awards for a sudden professional crisis, and you apply when the need arises rather than by a deadline. Fiscal sponsorship is different: a fiscal sponsor is a nonprofit that lets you receive grants and tax-deductible donations for a project without starting your own nonprofit, usually in exchange for a small percentage. If you ever find a grant that requires nonprofit status, a fiscal sponsor is how an individual composer can qualify.

  • FCA Emergency Grants: quick support for a sudden opportunity or crisis.
  • MusiCares: health and financial assistance for people who work in music.
  • Fractured Atlas: fiscal sponsorship and business tools for individual artists.
  • The Field: fiscal sponsorship for performing artists.
  • New York Foundation for the Arts (NYFA): fiscal sponsorship and a large national listing of opportunities.

residencies

A residency gives you time and space to work: a stretch of days or weeks away from your normal life, often with a room, a studio, and meals, sometimes funded and sometimes for a fee. For a composer that means uninterrupted time to write, plus a community of other artists. Res Artis is an international directory you can search by country and discipline. MacDowell and Yaddo are two of the best-known in the United States.

  • Res Artis: a searchable worldwide directory of artist residencies.
  • MacDowell: a major US residency open to composers.
  • Yaddo: a major US residency open to composers.

staying current

Reading, listening, and courses to keep up with the field and keep learning on your own time.

read and listen

  • I Care If You Listen: a magazine on new and experimental music.
  • NewMusicBox: New Music USA's long-running magazine, written largely by composers.
  • The Portfolio Composer: a podcast on the working and business life of a composer.

free and low-cost courses

  • edX: university courses online, including Berklee's music offerings.
  • FutureLearn: online courses, with a music and audio catalog.
  • Kadenze: courses in music technology and creative coding.
  • Open Culture: a directory of free online courses and cultural media.
  • The Musician's Way: a site on practice, performance, and the creative process.
