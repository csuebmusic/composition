the composition portfolio

What to build across your studies, how to organize it, and how to submit it each semester.
Inés Thiebaut · CSU East Bay

Composition is juried on a portfolio rather than a recital. Across your studies you build and maintain a body of work, the composition faculty review it at the end of every semester, and what you have written, alongside how the portfolio is growing, determines whether you advance. This is for undergraduate and graduate composers. Music producers have a separate guide.

what the portfolio is

Your Creative Portfolio is the collected body of work you write during your studies, kept in one organized place and updated every semester. Unlike a performance major, you are not judged on a single recital. You are judged on this growing portfolio, which is reviewed at a jury at the end of each semester.

At that jury you meet with the composition faculty, who look at what you finished during the semester and at the portfolio as a whole. Those reviews decide your retention and advancement in the program, so the portfolio is the center of your progress rather than something you assemble at the last minute. It is also the first professional record of your work, the thing you show to ensembles, schools, and competitions after you graduate.

what your portfolio contains

Two things travel with every work: a score and a recording. Alongside the works, the portfolio always holds a current CV and a short biography.

every work

For each piece, include the score as a PDF of the master score, meaning the full score rather than the individual parts. If a work is electronic and has no score, include a short technical document in its place. Include a recording of the work as well. A MIDI realization is acceptable as a stand-in, but only until the piece has a real performance recording, at which point the recording replaces the mock-up.

always in the portfolio

A CV, focused on your education and your music (how to write one). And a short biography in two parts: an introduction page, and an elevator pitch of a few sentences that say who you are as a composer and what your music is about.

if you are an undergraduate

You graduate with at least two works for every semester of applied lessons you take: one substantial work and one shorter work each semester. Over your degree that accumulates into a full portfolio. Every work still needs its score and its recording.

if you are a graduate

Your portfolio is built from six works, and every one of them is publicly performed and recorded:

  • Two chamber works of ten minutes or longer, recorded by the professional ensemble the area brings in each spring to perform and record student work.
  • A sound piece for fixed electronics, with or without visuals.
  • An electroacoustic piece for a student performer, ideally a graduate colleague, with fixed or live electronics.
  • A choir piece, written for a CSUEB choir.
  • A chamber orchestra piece, written for the CSUEB chamber orchestra.

Only the two chamber works carry a length minimum. The choir and chamber orchestra pieces may run shorter than ten minutes.

Your portfolio is reviewed at the progress juries, levels 6a through 6c, where the faculty track its growth and quality. The degree ends with the level 6d capstone: about four weeks ahead, you present the capstone lecture to the composition faculty and submit the capstone paper, and once it is cleared, the lecture is scheduled as a public presentation (the capstone).

file types and formats

The portfolio is an archive, so its files need to last and to open cleanly on any machine the faculty use. Two formats do most of the work: PDF for anything on the page, and lossless audio for recordings.

scores as PDF

Export every score as a PDF from your notation software (Dorico, Sibelius, Finale, MuseScore). A PDF fixes the layout, so your engraving looks the same everywhere and nothing reflows on someone else's screen. Export the master score, the full score with all instruments, rather than the separate performance parts. Give one PDF per work, open it yourself to check that every page, symbol, and font renders correctly, and embed fonts if your program offers the option.

electronic works with no score

When a piece is electronic and there is no notated score, a short technical document takes the score's place. Write it as a PDF and include what someone would need to understand and present the work: the setup and instrumentation, the software and any hardware, the signal flow, the patch or session structure, the duration, and what a performance requires. Think of it as the map that lets your piece be understood and reproduced without you in the room.

recordings as lossless audio

Save recordings in a lossless format. Lossless means the file keeps the full audio with nothing thrown away, unlike MP3 or AAC, which shrink the file by discarding sound the format guesses you will not miss. For an archive you want the full-quality version, so use WAV, FLAC, or AIFF. WAV is the safe universal choice, and FLAC is a good option when you want smaller files at the same quality. Aim for at least 44.1 kHz and 16-bit, with 48 kHz and 24-bit better when your recording supports it. Export a stereo mix from your DAW. Keep an MP3 only for casual sharing, never as the portfolio copy.

your CV and biography

Save your CV and your biography as PDFs too, for the same reason your scores are PDFs: the formatting stays put. Keep your editable original, the Word or Pages file, so you can update it each semester and export a fresh PDF into the portfolio when you do.

easy to get wrong

Two things trip people up. Submit the master score rather than the individual parts. Save recordings as lossless audio rather than MP3. Both keep the portfolio at archival quality.

Scores
PDF, master score only.
Electronic works without a score
PDF technical document.
Recordings
Lossless audio: WAV, FLAC, or AIFF. A MIDI realization stands in only until a performance recording exists.
CV and biography
PDF.

organizing your files

A portfolio is only as useful as it is easy to open and read in order. Keep one folder for your whole portfolio, one subfolder per work, numbered so they sort in the order you want the faculty to see them. Name every file so that its composer, its work, and what it is are clear from the name alone.

A workable structure looks like this:

Lastname_Firstname_Portfolio/
  01_WorkTitle/
    Lastname_WorkTitle_score.pdf
    Lastname_WorkTitle_recording.wav
  02_WorkTitle/
    Lastname_WorkTitle_score.pdf
    Lastname_WorkTitle_recording.wav
  03_ElectronicWork/
    Lastname_ElectronicWork_notes.pdf
    Lastname_ElectronicWork_audio.wav
  CV/
    Lastname_CV.pdf
  Bio/
    Lastname_bio.pdf

A few rules keep the names clean and sortable:

  • Number each work folder with two digits (01, 02, 03) so they hold their order. Two digits keep 02 ahead of 10.
  • Skip spaces in file and folder names. Join words with an underscore or a capital letter, since spaces and characters like slashes, colons, and quotation marks can break on other systems.
  • Put the same information in every file name, in the same order: your last name, the work, and what the file is (score, recording, notes).
  • Keep capitalization and spelling consistent, so a title reads the same way everywhere it appears.

submitting on Canvas

At the end of every semester you submit your whole portfolio on Canvas as a single compressed file, a zip. Zipping bundles the entire folder into one file you upload in one step, and it keeps your folder structure and file names intact for whoever opens it.

make the zip

On a Mac, right-click the top portfolio folder and choose Compress. On Windows, right-click the folder and choose Send to, then Compressed (zipped) folder. Either one produces a single .zip next to your folder. Name it with your last name, the word portfolio, and the semester, for example Lastname_Portfolio_Fall2026.zip.

check it before you submit

Open your own zip before you upload it. Unzip it into a new place and confirm that every work is present, every PDF opens, and every recording plays. A zip that is missing a file, or that holds a score that will not open, is the most common problem at submission, and testing your own copy first catches it.

Keep your organized portfolio folder on your own computer, and back it up somewhere else as well. Canvas holds what you submit each semester, and the composition area keeps the reviewed portfolio in its shared storage, but the maintained copy on your own machine is the one you keep building on.

keeping it current

Update the portfolio as you go, not the week before the jury. When you finish a work, add its score and its recording right away, while the files are in front of you. When a piece is finally performed, replace the MIDI mock-up with the performance recording. Keep your master files, in the native formats of your notation software and your DAW, so you can revise and re-export later.

Treat the portfolio as a public document, because it becomes one. The same organized, clearly labeled, well-recorded set of works that carries you through the program is what you send to ensembles, festivals, and graduate schools when you leave.
